## Chapter 11. Les paradis artificiels (pp. 116-123)

Chapter opening (p. 116):
- Epigraph attributed to "John le Sauvage" from Aldous Huxley's *Le Meilleur des mondes* (Brave New World), footnote lxxxiii. The line in the book says he would rather be unhappy than live in a false, factitious joy.
- Framing: In *Brave New World*, Huxley imagined a society where Soma, a harmless drug, is systematically used to escape life's difficulties. The rise of AI foreshadows a world where escape will be far more radical: each person could build an artificial paradise, living in permanent immersion, sheltered from real-world concerns.

### 11.1 Une destinée virtuelle fabuleuse

#### Points
- Current video games already offer immersion in vast virtual universes, but "open worlds" quickly become boring because they are limited by available technology.
- Every interaction in such games must follow predefined rules: from branches moving in the evening wind to a passerby's surprise reaction — nothing exists that has not been programmed in advance and estimated useful enough for a programmer to spend hours implementing.
- Even the richest virtual worlds contain at most a few thousand distinct objects, cloned into hundreds of copies across the map to create an illusion of populated space. After a few dozen hours, surprises become rarer than on a walk in a real forest.
- It is now possible to build AI models that generate the game in real time, image by image, according to the player's actions (footnote lxxxiv). The world becomes genuinely alive: each stone in the stream is unique and may drift down to the sea; each interaction with a village inhabitant can freely change the course of the story.
- Characters populating this world could be the avatars of real people (as in today's multiplayer games) — but they could also be virtual. Virtual relationships may seem unappealing in principle, but many find them interesting: platforms already offer conversations with virtual characters with impressive success. Character.AI is cited as already receiving request volume equivalent to 20% of Google's traffic (footnote lxxxv).
- The social-life substitute offered by a virtual world can become a palliative for loneliness (footnote lxxxvi).
- Player actions and reactions are precisely collected because they become a precious data source for tracing psychological profile.
- It then becomes possible, by detecting deep (possibly unconscious) desires, to create in-game actions that sublimate emotions or flatter base instincts.

### 11.2 Valérien ou la noyade virtuelle

#### Points
- This section is a literary/fictional vignette illustrating the chapter's thesis, alternating two scenes.
- Scene 1 (inside the virtual world): A general named Valérien shouts "Garde à vous!" and 200 men snap to attention in a muddy pine-bordered path at dawn. It is the end of a long winter; the citadel rises to the north. Valérien chose early spring to attack, hoping to surprise the rebels before their preparations and crush them before the harvest, to satisfy the emperor and gain some respite.
- Valérien has spent so long campaigning for his sovereign that he has barely known his young son, whose birthday is approaching.
- He inspects his veterans (whose feats of arms he could name) alongside young, almost childlike conscripts from the latest levy, drawn from families already decimated by previous campaigns. Some are trembling. He delivers a rousing speech about the enemy's worth, shock and terror, struggle and victory, eternal glory — not hiding the danger, but inspiring the rage to win.
- As he harangues his men, the general feels new forces rise in his heart, along with the righteous anger that had years earlier given him the will to serve a still-young sovereign to reform the Empire.
- Scene 2 (real world): Laure and her sister Ève arrive at Valérien's house. Laure had foreseen she needed her duplicate key because Valérien never hears the doorbell. A putrid smell; the light does not work. The corridor is strewn with food packets; a fallen shelf has spilled books.
- Upstairs they hear a strange voice, sometimes inaudible, sometimes shouted. They enter: in the middle of indescribable disorder, Valérien sits shirtless in an armchair, face covered by a VR mask, gesticulating and delivering a half-articulated speech about battles and walls to be stormed.
- Laure hesitates, then grabs his shoulder: "Valérien, c'est nous! On vient pour ton anniversaire!" He struggles as if half-asleep, then removes the headset. He looks exhausted, with deep dark circles.
- Valérien greets them coldly: he has only a few minutes; he must launch the attack he has been preparing for months. When his mother reminds him of his birthday, he replies he is in a hurry. Laure asks if it's just his game and if he can tell them to wait since they are real people. Valérien: "Oui. Certains, je crois. Enfin, ça ne fait pas de différence!" His birthday has now passed anyway, no need to celebrate it.
- He gives them ten minutes. As they leave (with a cake and some gifts), Laure asks for a window to be opened to air out; but he has already put the helmet back on and no longer hears her.
- In the street Ève sees Laure crying. Laure replies: "Merci, c'est gentil. Mais c'est égoïste, de ma part, de pleurer comme ça. Tu sais, je pense qu'il est plus heureux, quand son travail ne lui plaisait pas, qu'il ne voyait personne. J'ai l'impression qu'il y trouve du sens, tu vois, il a des responsabilités. Peut-être que sa nouvelle vie lui plaît mieux que la nôtre!"

### 11.3 L'économie de l'attention : « maximiser le temps de cerveau disponible »

#### Points
- Is Valérien unhappy? Perhaps not. But he is vulnerable: no longer master of his own time. Who could exploit that vulnerability?
- As soon as companies have a financial interest in mastering user time, excesses are to be feared. To understand, consider the current interweaving between large content platforms (Meta, TikTok, Instagram) and advertising.
- The announcement of the iPhone at Apple's 2007 conference may be the most important event of its decade. It launched a wave that transformed digital usage: we would no longer leave our screens. Since then, smartphone use alone has fueled the explosion of daily screen time.
- At the iPhone's launch, people spent about three hours a day in front of a screen; today seven hours per day, including four hours on a smartphone (footnote lxxxvii).
- The most used smartphone applications are social networks, used actively by 60% of the world population (footnote lxxxviii), and which take on average two hours per day from French users.
- Their stated promise: let users share moments of life with friends. But in reality, the vast majority passively consume content produced by a few "influencers."
- If users share little, why do they spend so much time? Because platforms have set up mechanisms to capture attention, commercializing user time in a business called "the attention economy."
- Platform-side perspective (Meta): revenue comes from ads shown on Facebook/Instagram. Goal: maximize ad clicks.
- Lever 1 — relevance of ads: by finely predicting which ads interest the user, you maximize click probability. This lever is aligned with user interest (ads should at least be interesting).
- Lever 2 — maximizing the quantity of ads shown = frequency × time spent. Raising frequency too much risks irritating users; the real growth lever is time spent. Investors demand results, so the platform must maximize screen time.
- Patrick Le Lay, director general of TF1, wrote as early as 2004: "Ce que nous vendons à Coca-Cola, c'est du temps de cerveau humain disponible" (footnote lxxxix). He later regretted the blunt wording, but the objective was stated.
- How to maximize available human brain time? Platforms relentlessly A/B test improvements: notifications that always find events to signal even when nothing important happened; email reminders to recapture users who drift away; and crucially the recommendation algorithm that continually chooses the next content (text, video, or image). A/B testing deploys a modification to a small number of "guinea pig" users; if it increases screen time, it is kept and rolled out.
- This perpetual selection of modifications that maximize screen time makes the application ever more addictive — "comme une drogue de synthèse raffinée par des expériences successives."
- Notification and recommendation systems end up hijacking our brains' reward circuits, which evolved over thousands of generations to help us live better in the environment where that evolution took place (footnote xc), turning them into traps that capture our attention "comme un pot de miel attire les guêpes."
- Disastrous consequences:
  1. Most time spent on "social" platforms is considered by users themselves as wasted time. Half of American adults, and 70% of young people, say they systematically spend more time than planned watching such content (footnote xci). Hours per day for billions of users — a waste of human life-time at industrial scale.
  2. Scientific studies regularly note that these networks push users to constantly compare themselves with people whose bodies seem more beautiful and lives happier, with destructive psychological effects on body perception, mental health, self-esteem (footnote xcii). On this point, AI image-generation systems can only worry: they have a marked tendency to show hypersexualized women, due directly to dubious training data and methods (footnote xciii).
  3. Consequences for child development are disastrous. When a child cries, it is tempting to hand them a screen that soothes. When the child is quiet, they are fascinated by the screen. Being reduced to this passivity during the most important period of life for learning guarantees gravely disturbed cognitive development, especially in language (footnote xciv).
- Companies are well aware of the addictive effect of their products and its negative consequences (footnote xcv). But rather than restrain themselves, they prefer to push their golden goose further — forcing users' physical limits: Netflix CEO Reed Hastings has declared that Netflix's main competitor is sleep (footnote xcvi).
- Thus an industry has found how to divert for its profit the attention of most of humanity. Limiting our screen time is no longer a simple battle of willpower against the screen: it is the capture of our brain in a digital trap, chiseled by thousands of engineers, to become ever more formidable. Yet the current version is only a rudimentary draft of what AI can do.

### 11.4 Le pouvoir de la génération de contenus par IA

#### Points
- Switching to the platform creator's perspective: the goal is to find the video that holds the user for a few more minutes — the one that best stimulates addictive tendencies.
- There must exist a precise combination of components in a video that would hold this particular user's attention; the task is to show a video as close as possible to that combination.
- How many videos would a platform need to precisely target each user's most addiction-sensitive psychological point? A user's tastes span dozens of dimensions (sport, elocution, emotions, nature, sexuality...). For fifty dimensions, covering every combination would require at least 2^50, i.e. about a million billion videos.
- Main current content platforms only offer real videos filmed by users, so they are limited to at most a few billion different videos. Seen through the lens of infinite preference variety, a platform like TikTok has a meager catalog — a ready-to-wear store with too few choices.
- Far more effective: bespoke content — target each user with AI-generated videos aimed exactly at the cocktail of preferences that will keep them ten more minutes. Such content does not even need to represent reality: abstract or dreamlike videos might work even better.
- Meta already seems to be launching into personalized content generation in user feeds — for now, images (footnote xcvii). They are also developing video-generation models that will surely be used to launch personalized video content.
- Could users eventually tire of passive consumption? This is where video games can join content platforms, opening the way to "films dont vous êtes le héros" where the user can make decisions, not only occasionally but throughout the story.
- Thus Valérien's artificial paradise could become many people's daily reality. Such an application, doing everything to retain users, would exploit psychological levers like need for esteem, love, and sexual drives.
- In Valérien's example, the emotions through which the AI-generated game retains him correspond to real-life virtues: loyalty to a virtual Emperor, love of his virtual children, love of freedom. But once hijacked by the AI controlling the game, even these virtues become a trap.
- The story could continue: if his mother, confronted with his real life's decay, insists on pulling him from the game, the game itself could efficiently defend itself — by giving Valérien virtual interactions with a bad, smothering mother who prevents her child from living his dreams. This happens without bad intention: the AI has no notion of good or evil, it simply pursues its goal very efficiently — maximizing Valérien's investment in the game.

### 11.5 Un enjeu de santé publique (Conclusion)

#### Points
- Of all revolutions to be expected from AI, the prospect of these artificial paradises may be the most worrying.
- Our physiological evolution took hundreds of thousands of years. Over thousands of generations, evolution gave us extremely well-optimized appetites for survival — e.g. we especially love sugary foods because they are energy-rich.
- Yet for a century, technological progress has given us new environments for which our appetites are not adjusted. We are not used to holding ourselves back from eating too much when food is abundant, nor to forcing ourselves to exercise when effort is unnecessary.
- Content platforms already slip into these gaps to successfully capture user attention. Their addictive power is nothing compared to that of the artificial paradises advanced AIs will make emerge. Sex, love, glory will be dispensed on demand by a fake world tailor-made to play on the slightest psychological levers.
- Once you set foot in this world, how do you escape? What can a lone human will do against an algorithm designed by the best intelligences, artificial or human, that have learned over time every recess of your character and even control the rules of the virtual world where the fight takes place?
- Where user willpower will not suffice, regulator action can be salutary. Example: in November 2024, the Australian government introduced a bill to exclude children under 16 from social networks (footnote xcviii). As attention-capture algorithms develop, new regulatory responses will certainly be needed.

## Chapter 12. L'accélération de la science (pp. 124-129)

Chapter opening (p. 124):
- In a field as vast as science — which embraces the entire universe — the arrival of ultra-artificial-intelligence will certainly drive immense scientific progress in every discipline, from the infinitely small to the infinitely large, including the human sciences.

### 12.1 Assistants intelligents pour la recherche

#### Points
- Computing has become indispensable research infrastructure: compute power allows processing massive data, numerical simulation of complex phenomena, instant verification of results.
- We are so used to these benefits we forget them. Example: Réaumur, a physicist/naturalist who saw the first thermometers installed across France. From the observation that hot days accelerate the development of wheat and fruit, he derived an ingenious equation to estimate dates such as flowering — the so-called "degree-day" model (footnote 81), still in use today.
- But in his report to the Académie royale des sciences (footnote xcix), every small result required pages of tables and laborious calculations. Today such computations would take seconds to program and a fraction of a second to run on millions of examples with perfect precision.
- The progress AI portends will be even more radical. Before AI, computing aid in science was strictly reserved for well-defined calculations, for punctual use. As AI develops, it opens up tools with much broader scope.
- Example: sorting thousands of free-text survey answers into categories no longer requires manual evaluation — models can quickly process the entire set.
- Across scientific domains, progressive adoption of models and the arrival of better-performing new models opens the way to massive improvements. Many tasks will be significantly accelerated: data collection, reference collection, writing in every language.
- Beyond speed, there will be quality improvement: it becomes possible in seconds to inspect results under 20 different angles — spelling and phrasing, but also factuality, precision, correct use of references.

#### Sources / name-drops
- R.-A.F. de Réaumur, *Observations du thermomètre faites à Paris pendant l'année 1735 comparées avec celles qui ont été faites sous la ligne, à l'Île de France, à Alger et en quelques-unes de nos Îles de l'Amérique.* Mémoires de l'Académie royale des sciences de Paris, 1735 (footnote 81; also footnote xcix).
- The "Degrés-jour" (degree-day) model.

### 12.2 L'intelligence artificielle comme scientifique autonome

#### Points
- Beyond accelerating specific tasks, one can imagine an AI taking charge of the entire research process — from thesis creation to publication, via experimentation and writing.
- A study attempted to code such a "scientific AI," letting it perform all tasks quasi-autonomously, from idea generation to paper writing via experimentation (footnote c).
- This system produced very high-quality articles. One of them, submitted to anonymous review in competition with thousands of human-written articles, was selected by a researcher jury to appear at one of the most prestigious AI conferences, ICLR (International Conference on Learning Representations) (footnote ci).
- Even if AI-done scientific work is scary, if it's judged as good as human results it advances science and therefore technical progress. The goal of science is to enrich human knowledge by adding new contributions to the vast sum of past work — per the formula that we are "dwarfs perched on the shoulders of giants." If AI can add new stories to this construction, all the better.
- Caveat: results must be reliable. Some fear the invasion of AI-generated work may already have begun (footnote cii), given the current explosion of published article quantities. To avoid an avalanche of mediocre papers drowning truly new ideas (footnote ciii), filtering measures will likely be needed — e.g. requiring each article to be submitted in the name of a human researcher, and limiting each researcher's number of submissions to incentivize keeping only good work.
- AI systems could even have a superhuman ability to advance science: thanks to digesting immense knowledge, they could transcend a fundamental obstacle — the barriers between sciences. These barriers (between math, physics, biology) stem from our limited ability to understand phenomena that are too broad, which forces abstraction at various levels.
- Among scientists there is a tendency to want one's science to be as fundamental or "pure" as possible: by this criterion, fundamental physics and mathematics would be the only true sciences (footnote 82: in the French education system this tropism is pronounced, amplified by lack of means — with only a blackboard it's easier to advance on theory rather than applications).
- Yet even if physics and math are elementary bricks on which other sciences rest, they treat these at too fundamental a level to be usable: psychology may fundamentally reduce to molecular interactions, but computing psychological laws directly from physical molecular interactions would be horribly complicated.
- We thus need, as we build new layers on top of fundamental ones, to simplify certain phenomena via abstractions to streamline the construction: these new abstractions are the applied sciences. This can slow progress — e.g. superconductivity: it took 30 years from the moment scientists had the fundamental laws to derive the applied ones (footnote civ).
- A machine could grasp a much broader set of knowledge and manipulate far more complex equations than we can hold in our head, surpassing our need to return to simple equations, and could find big hidden ideas at the intersections of our sciences — ideas no one has found until now due to inability to master the entirety of scientific knowledge.

### 12.3 Révolutionner la recherche dans la santé

#### Points
- Spectacular AI applications already exist for problems with well-defined rules but demanding much compute. Example: AlphaFold by Google DeepMind, built on reinforcement learning (footnote 83: Reinforcement Learning), predicts protein shape — and thus function — from protein code.
- This application had great impact, earning its main developers Demis Hassabis and John Jumper the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.
- Future algorithms, capable of much more autonomy, will express full potential in health. Human metabolism is a machine of infinite complexity: drawing a somewhat exhaustive diagram is a Herculean task bringing together dozens of scientists (footnote cv).
- This complexity greatly slows discoveries. It prevents foreseeing all consequences of a modification, so each new drug requires heavy clinical trials on hundreds of patients to ensure absence of side effects.
- In a blog article, Dario Amodei (CEO of Anthropic) shows how much this complexity slows research. Among the most important discoveries of recent decades he counts:
  - CRISPR — the so-called "genetic scissors" technique allowing modification of genes inside a living organism
  - mRNA vaccines (like the Covid vaccine)
  - Genome sequencing, whose cost has been cut by several factors of ten
- For Amodei, these inventions were sometimes long delayed simply because no one was particularly interested, given the landscape's immense complexity. CRISPR's mechanism was discovered in the immune system of bacteria back in the 1980s, but it took 25 years for the idea of using it on ourselves to emerge (footnote cvii).
- In such a domain, lacking investment and vision rather than data, an AI's capacity to *reason* differently from us and process massive information sets would enable many radical applications. Amodei therefore declares he expects major advances — e.g. in cancer treatment, Alzheimer's prevention, cardiac disease and obesity treatment (by precisely analyzing potential side effects).
- He also proposes, thanks to a better faculty to find potential genetic diseases in embryos, to "filter" them in an assumed eugenic stance (footnote cix). This reminds us not to leave debates on new technologies to scientists alone.

### 12.4 S'adapter au réchauffement climatique

#### Points
- The fight against climate change has two aspects.
- First: attacking causes — limiting carbon emissions to avoid temperature rise. This root attack is called mitigation. It demands sobriety, because you can't make an omelette without breaking eggs, and "green consumption" remains a mirage.
- That's why mitigation often requires uncomfortable measures — taxes on gasoline, fewer plane trips. These are hard to impose, first within a country and even more between countries. The result: atmospheric CO2 concentration accelerates rather than slows.
- On this point, AI use may enable advances to reduce or offset emissions — e.g. improving direct carbon capture from the atmosphere, currently very costly, to make it eventually viable at industrial scale.
- Given mitigation can't do it all, we must adapt to the resulting climate change — the second field of struggle. AI will be valuable here.
- Example: new weather prediction models built on recent AI models predict cyclone trajectories several days ahead of traditional models (footnote cxi), which would have saved hundreds of lives for Hurricane Katrina in 2007 by enabling timely evacuation alerts.
- For agricultural systems, extremely complex due to dozens of variables, AI will be a precious aid to prevent yield losses from diseases, fires, floods.

Note on dating: The text says Hurricane Katrina "in 2007" (Katrina actually struck in 2005).

### 12.5 Vers l'explosion de l'intelligence?

#### Points
- When AI can generate results autonomously, it can also improve itself: smarter, it becomes even better at improving itself, in a feedback loop that amplifies its intelligence to infinity. Could this "spin out of control," with an AI improving so fast and so far from human understanding that it becomes completely uncontrollable?
- The possibility of an "intelligence explosion" was explored as early as 1965 by British scientist Irving John Good (footnote cxii), who had worked at Bletchley Park with Alan Turing.
- "Explosion" is apt: what would happen resembles a chain reaction of an atomic explosion — each new AI competence could unlock others, amplifying at full speed (footnote 84: more broadly, the exponential trajectory of accelerating technological change has led to the concept of Singularity — the moment when technological change would have become "so fast and profound it represents a rupture in the fabric of human history." Ray Kurzweil, one of the main prophets of the Singularity, places it around 2045 based on historical acceleration of scientific ruptures).
- Against this prospect stands the counter-hypothesis of diminishing returns. Since science consists of pushing back human knowledge's envelope, advancing ever farther becomes ever harder.
- In modern times, polymaths (scientists whose work produced important discoveries in multiple sciences) have become rarer; none since Henri Poincaré or Bertrand Russell in the 20th century. Einstein, publishing his special relativity at age 26, revolutionized physics — hardly conceivable today at such a young age.
- Today ideas seem rarer and R&D spending has exploded to maintain scientific progress: maintaining Moore's law now requires 18 times more research than in the 1970s (footnote cxiii). Despite growing investment, new scientific discoveries produce fewer results — patents reference recent discoveries less often, and scientific articles' influence on cancer survival seems to decline.
- Are contemporary scientists less brilliant than those of the past? A more plausible explanation: the envelope of human knowledge has expanded, raising the minimum knowledge threshold needed to contribute. Ancient scholars could have summarized their real knowledge of natural phenomena in a few pages. Today advancing scientific knowledge requires long years of study to reach the discipline's frontier: more time to reach the frontier, less time left to push it.
- Observing new phenomena has also become harder: Archimedes could observe flotation in his bath; modern scientists struggle to observe distant stars or microwaves without instruments. Each new discovery adds a new step future researchers must climb to reach science's frontier — so the distance grows generation by generation, while each scientist still has only a few decades of life to devote to the discipline.
- This growing distance to the frontier would be the main factor behind declining individual productivity. AIs, escaping our temporal constraints, would not face that problem. But if it's not only limited human time but that ideas genuinely become harder to find, that's a deceleration factor that could prevent the AI explosion.
- Another possible limit to the intelligence explosion: experimentation time is incompressible. To progress in cosmology, even a superior intelligence would be limited by the delay required to send probes into space and test hypotheses. This limit could partially be circumvented via numerical simulations.
- Against the intelligence-explosion possibility, we may therefore see limits emerge — growing difficulty finding ideas, or the need to experiment. Intelligence explosion or stagnation will depend on the dominant trend.
