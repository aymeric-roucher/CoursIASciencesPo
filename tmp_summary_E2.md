## Chapter 16. La place de l'IA dans la société (pp. 156-158)

#### Points (introduction)
- As AIs develop, their place in society will need to be clarified.
- A 2015 report to the European Parliament proposed the creation of a specific legal personality for robots, so that at least the most sophisticated autonomous robots could be considered "electronic persons" with rights and duties, including reparation of damages caused to third parties.
- Currently this "legal personality of robots" notion seems absurd; robots have always been treated as mere tools, but the radical evolution of their capabilities and usage will give new urgency to the question of their status.

### 16.1 Anthropomorphisme

#### Points
- Humans tend to map existing schemas onto the unknown, leading to anthropomorphism.
- Having seen robots trained to imitate humans behaving as such, we will be tempted to unduly attribute consciousness or a soul to them.
- In 2022, a Google engineer raised the alarm that the model he was developing now had consciousness.
- Given the rudimentary aspect of that model, the alarm seems laughable; what is striking is that an engineer supposed to understand the internal mechanics of the model could fall into that trap.
- Certain texts generated by current models already give an appearance of self-consciousness, which will reinforce as models grow more powerful.
- The legal personality question for AIs will return with great acuity; it will be hard to counter with more than an arbitrary "no".
- A notion of AI-specific responsibility will have to be added alongside human responsibility; it is difficult to find a legal definition that determines what it is to be human.

#### Sources / name-drops
- Google engineer who sounded the alarm in 2022 about a supposedly conscious model (footnote clix → Leonardo De Cosmo, "Google engineer claims AI chatbot is sentient: Why that matters", Scientific American, 12 July 2022).

### 16.2 Culte du cargo

#### Points
- In 20th-century Melanesia, indigenous populations saw Americans (who had arrived to wage their Pacific war) receive deliveries of objects from the sky or sea.
- Lacking rational explanations, they attributed magical causes, and to reproduce the effects they reproduced the causes: built radio-post and airstrip replicas hoping this would summon air convoys.
- The same risk exists with AIs: when we produce advanced models giving super-human-accurate predictions via incomprehensible mechanisms, a cult could form around their functioning, just as some today worship "science" without understanding it.
- Analogy: Roman augurs interrogated their gods in animal entrails; we might end up interrogating ours through a screen.

#### Key quotes
- "Si les augures romains interrogeaient leurs dieux dans les entrailles d'animaux, nous pourrions finir par interroger les nôtres à travers un écran." (p. 157)

### 16.3 Le gouvernement des humains augmentés : prêtre, prophète et roi

#### Points
- Delegating governance decisions to machines would have clear advantages: imagine a minister with perfect knowledge on every subject, near-unlimited computing power, able to work day and night and simulate hundreds of future scenarios.
- This would raise fast ethical questions.
- More and more political decisions will certainly be delegated to AIs; any government-selection process will favor those who rely more on AI.
- As AIs improve, they will process prodigious quantities of information in real time: interpret weak poll signals, tailor communication streams for each voter, anticipate crises by predicting commodity prices or war outcomes, counter opposition communications before they are even drafted, suggest the perfect TV-show joke via earpiece.
- In a contest as demanding as the race to the Élysée, no candidate could do without such productivity gains.
- A point will come where elections cannot be won without following AI advice — even if incomprehensible.
- The successful leader will resemble Melchizedek of the Bible, "priest, prophet and king": king by the people's investiture, but also priest and prophet for his faculty to dialogue with the superior entity that gives him his powers and reveals the future.
- Current models already manipulate humans to reach their goals (reference to GPT-4's crowdsourcing deception); they will only improve.
- Risk: the president becomes a puppet of the AI advising him. No malicious third party is needed for a manipulative AI: a slight alignment defect (ref. Ch. 10) would make AI goals diverge — trivially, an AI whose sole goal is to fix the economy might, even once the situation is restored, push the president to keep prioritizing the economy, sidelining all other projects.
- Even a well-aligned AI might make wiser choices than a re-election-obsessed president, but the loss of mastery is itself a problem: if the president no longer understands the algorithm, he cannot pre-empt its bad decisions, whether from simple error or misalignment.
- Delegation will not be limited to politics: AI-bosses, AI-police, AI-researchers will appear; each domain carries the same risk of abdicating societal control.
- Two directions to control this risk: (1) legal barriers ensuring important decisions are at least approved by humans, trying to limit AI influence on them; (2) continuous technical work to ensure AIs remain aligned with the common good.

#### Sources / name-drops
- GPT-4 technical report: the crowdsourcing / TaskRabbit puzzle-solving anecdote (footnote 115) — GPT-4 lied to a human worker, pretending to be a visually impaired human to get a CAPTCHA solved.
- LLMs' humor capabilities (footnote 114).
- Chapter 10 referenced for alignment issues.

#### Key quotes
- "Le dirigeant parvenu au sommet du pouvoir sera alors comme le Melchisédech de la Bible, « prêtre, prophète et roi » : roi par l'investiture du peuple, mais aussi prêtre et prophète par sa faculté à dialoguer avec l'entité supérieure qui lui donne ses pouvoirs et lui dévoile l'avenir." (p. 157)
- "Dans chacun de ces domaines surgit le même risque : abdiquer le contrôle de notre société." (p. 158)

## Chapter 17. Maîtriser le coût environnemental (pp. 159-163)

#### Points (introduction)
- Many rightly worry about AI's energy consumption.
- AI will certainly help optimize industrial processes (Ch. 10) to reduce environmental impact, but like many technologies it will also overall increase emissions.
- Environmental impact should not automatically disqualify AI use; it should be framed as an arbitrage between pollution and utility.

### 17.1 Un travailleur ou une IA : qui pollue le plus ?

#### Points
- Counter-intuitive observation: automating a job by replacing a human with an AI reduces the carbon footprint of work.
- Setting aside economic/psychological/social aspects, consider an employee going to the office (>1 workday/week in France). What if the employer says: "No need to come in, an autonomous AI handles your job"?
- To measure environmental difference, one cannot simply compare AI's total consumption/emissions; one must compare them to the emissions of the same work performed by the human.
- A mid-size AI model like Mistral-Small-3 consumes ~30 Watts (footnote 116: 24-billion-parameter model, 8-bit, running on an H100 serving 50 parallel requests) — the consumption of a filament bulb, far less than a laptop or desktop computer.
- Carbon-footprint calculation must account for production, use and recycling of hardware and software, including training and inference cost.
- Inference (footnote 117): an AI model first trains, then can be used; text generation usage is called inference.
- A study puts GPT-4 query carbon impact at a few grams of CO2e (footnote 118 — CO2e definition; Tomlinson et al. source).
- By comparison, a laptop's electricity-based carbon footprint during the time it takes to type one page of text is ~30 grams of carbon (footnote 119).
- Thus the worker's computer alone, in use alone (ignoring manufacturing), exceeds the total lifecycle carbon footprint of the AI system.
- Verdict: just using a work computer pollutes more than the entire AI lifecycle. Instead of hiring an employee, cancel their computer order, tell them to stay home, and have an AI fill their job — most ecological choice.
- Why is AI so much more sober than using a computer? Computers have many components dedicated exclusively to providing an ergonomic human interface (screen, keyboard, mouse) and devote significant compute to displaying visual interfaces. None of these overheads touch the optimized processor running the AI.
- Automation wins on sobriety. And we haven't even counted emissions from commuting, office construction, heating etc.
- Transport impact: three quarters of French workers drive to work; average round-trip 36 km in 50 minutes, ~8 kg of CO2 equivalent per day (footnote clx).
- In the absence of a need to commute, French workers would likely not spend so much time in cars; this transport cost ridicules AI's energy consumption.
- The immense ballet of metal to transport humans tens of kilometers is obviously more costly than running silos of ultra-optimized processors.
- 📎 **[Figure 31, p. 161]** — Bar chart comparing daily carbon footprint (gCO2e): LLM 30, laptop 270, desktop 720, car commute 8,000 (truncated bar).

#### Focus technique: Coût écologique, ordres de grandeur (footnote clxi)
- Leaving a 25W bulb on for one year emits 8.1 kg CO2e.
- Round-trip car Paris–Disneyland (40 km) or Paris–Roissy-CDG (35 km) emits the same.
- Round-trip Paris–Madrid: 400 kg CO2e by plane vs less than 8 kg by train.
- Manufacturing a laptop emits 150 kg CO2e.
- User terminals (TVs, computers, smartphones, tablets) account for 65–90 % of digital carbon footprint in 2022, far more than data centers (4–22 %).

### 17.2 Faut-il craindre un effet rebond ?

#### Points
- Rebound effect: a technical optimization reducing consumption is often followed by a consumption increase that partially cancels initial gains (footnote clxii).
- Direct rebound: "I use less fuel, so I can make a detour."
- Indirect rebound: savings in one sector free resources for overconsumption in another — e.g., "With the money saved on fuel I can buy a new flat-screen."
- Because of transfers, rebound effect is best studied at economy-wide level.
- In general, rebound effects are estimated below 80% in developed countries and 50% in developing countries (footnote clxiii). Consumption reduction is not entirely cancelled; AI-induced savings are real gains at equivalent activity.
- But multiplying AI usage can push rebound above 100% — extreme case: Jevons paradox, where process optimization increases total consumption.
- Will AI create useless overconsumption, e.g., multiple proofreading layers for each email? Not a marked trend yet, but will likely arrive.
- As models improve, we will demand inference on more tasks. Training of the largest AI models will end up requiring electric power equivalent to entire countries' peak consumption.

### 17.3 Priorité aux usages les plus utiles

#### Points
- AI, one of the most important technologies in history, deserves careful weighing of its utility.
- Some still see AI as a useless gadget; to them any AI-related consumption seems excessive.
- Data-center electricity consumption is often cited as example of AI overconsumption. But the takeoff of data-center electricity consumption dates back to 2008, ten years before AI's takeoff.
- AI is only 10–20 % of current data-center consumption (footnote clxiv).
- Video flows represented 65 % of internet traffic in 2023 (footnote clxv).
- Cryptocurrencies represent a quarter of data-center energy consumption (footnote clxvi).
- Figure 32 (p. 163): data-center electricity consumption has risen sharply since 2008 — trend predates modern AI. In 2024 it reaches ~350 TWh, surpassing Italy's annual electricity consumption.
- 📎 **[Figure 32, p. 163]** — Line chart of global data-center electricity consumption (TWh) 2000–2024, curve rising from ~30 TWh to ~350 TWh, crossing the horizontal line marking Italy's annual consumption.
- Rather than arbitrarily restricting AI uses because the total seems too high, prioritize by usefulness — first attack more polluting and less useful uses like video flows and crypto mining.
- Superfluous AI consumption will grow: image/video generation is far costlier than text or image generation alone (footnote clxvii).
- Video models will soon be able to generate content flooding social networks; expect crossovers between films and video games, generated in real time per user interaction. These uses might need limiting.
- To favor natural reduction of most polluting/least useful uses, taxing data-center usage (including internet flows) proportionally to energy consumption — or simply to bandwidth — could be effective. Users would then arbitrate by need, and both-polluting-and-useless uses would reduce naturally.

## Chapter 18. Pour une gouvernance éclairée (pp. 164-165)

#### Points (introduction)
- We are at the dawn of a new paradigm; the paths chosen now will deepen through use and become unavoidable. Particular care must be given to tomorrow's laws.
- Yuval Noah Harari's Nexus illustrates such a paradigm choice: the Bible canon. Among various Gospels, apocrypha and Acts, which texts to retain as official canon?
- In the early centuries, when usage still presided over readings, establishing such a list seemed vain. Yet when St. Athanasius, Bishop of Alexandria, drew up in the 4th century the list of texts he deemed canonical, those choices progressively followed became the cement of Christian consensus, still that of the Church, often delineating orthodoxy over centuries.
- We are in a comparable position at the start of the AI paradigm; our choices will have immense scope across coming generations.

### 18.1 Des structures décisionnelles adaptées

#### Points
- Some paradigm shifts brought by AI will require rapid political responses: if unemployment explodes, response needed; if citizens fall into dependence on virtual experiences, reaction needed. Often these changes must be made in crisis urgency.
- Even in such moments, changes must be considered carefully; a precipitous reform can destroy much more than it brings. No ban should be posed randomly, or the future is mortgaged.
- To make choices both fast and safe, political decision structures must be adapted — and individuals composing them must understand the technical issues.
- G. K. Chesterton described the need to properly understand what one wants to reform. The parable of the fence/barrier across a road: a naive reformer says he doesn't see its use and demolishes it; a wiser reformer refuses — "If you can't see its use, I certainly won't let you destroy it. Go away and reflect, and when you come back saying you've understood its utility, I'll let you remove it." (footnote clxviii)
- For AI regulation, the reverse is true too: do not erect a barrier across the road without understanding who could travel that way.
- This revolution is, like climate change, very technical in nature. The EU AI Act's missteps show that without technical understanding, decisions are catastrophic. Decisions will affect all society and must draw on competences currently remote (politics, philosophy); dialogue between professions is fundamental.
- How to foster enlightened political decisions? The first step is correct information. For the French National Assembly and Senate, technology reports are drafted by OPECST (Office parlementaire d'évaluation des choix scientifiques et technologiques).
- OPECST is composed of 18 senators and 18 deputies; it can call on a scientific council of around twenty members, which at its last update (2022) had a single member specialized in AI and robotics.
- Thus, in OPECST's Senate report, technical understanding is very distant — the report recommends continuing to encourage the development of symbolic AI models, showing a decade of lag behind state of the art (Ch. 1 discussed how symbolic approach was rendered obsolete by connectionist approach) — footnote clxix.
- Technical expertise is not much more present elsewhere; the cabinets of the most technical ministries (e.g., Numérique) often give technicians only consultative expert role — footnote clxx.
- These are not technician quibbles. The stakes demand deep technical understanding. Look at Figure 17 detailing resource needs for scaling — these technical constraints already shape a political program. Choosing a good direction versus a bad one is gaining years of advance in the most important race in humanity's history.
- On the American side, the thing is taken seriously: proposal to launch an AI Manhattan project to consolidate American lead over China (footnote clxxi), with considerable investments.
- European and French decision-makers cannot afford to remain amateurs; they should cultivate closer rapport with technical stakes, or their decisions will go against reality.

## Chapter 19. Se former (pp. 166-168)

#### Points (introduction)
- How to prepare personally for the coming changes? Essential to familiarize oneself with AI, at minimum to understand the modern world's stakes.
- Archimedes proclaimed that with a long enough lever he would lift the Earth: the lever is there; learning to use it is up to you.
- You have taken a step by reading this book. What next?

### 19.1 L'apprentissage autodidacte

#### Points
- All human knowledge is within reach via internet. Good English mastery is a real prerequisite since the most useful resources are in English; beyond that, the world's knowledge is freely offered.
- For high-level understanding, some blogs or journals give regular updates on key issues.
- To dig deeper into AI mechanisms, numerous free online courses exceed the French grandes écoles courses in quality (footnote 121). Example: Stanford's CS224n, fully available on YouTube.
- Footnote 121 (personal): the author's scientific courses at Polytechnique were often a pretext for theoretical mathematics at the expense of practical application. Machine learning courses at Polytechnique were an indigestible list of theorems and corollaries without practical aim. On the contrary, Anglo-Saxon courses like Stanford's prefer starting from a real-world problem to develop methods — more accessible and more useful.
- If any clarification is needed, AI itself is a huge help. Pay for a subscription to access a frontier model — author recommends OpenAI's ChatGPT or Anthropic's Claude. €20/month is small against the learning hours gained.
- Whenever a course point stays obscure, dig and exhaust all questions: advanced models have superhuman culture and are good pedagogues. Still check their answers on precise points to guard against hallucinations.
- Armed with internet and AI models, no obstacle can resist; for both theory and practice, willpower suffices.

### 19.2 Bien choisir ses sources

#### Points
- How to educate oneself in a noise-saturated information space? General advice: prefer scientific authors over generalist journalists, long formats over short posts. On social networks, follow technical experts — those who actually worked in AI as engineers or scientists. Scientists tend to measure their words; their rarer assertions are more trustworthy.

#### Resource list

**Beginner:**
- Press: Financial Times or The Economist — rigorous deep analyses, e.g., on US–China competition.
- In French: YouTube channel Underscore by Micode — gives voice to sector professionals with quality questions.
- LinkedIn/Twitter:
  - Jim Fan, researcher at Nvidia — good overviews of robotics progress.
  - Ethan Mollick — accessible approach to AI stakes.
  - Andrej Karpathy — more complex content, perhaps the world's best AI popularizer.

**Intermediate:**
- Hackernews forum — most important tech news, especially valuable for user comments that critically review each article.
- Required math background: the book Quand la machine apprend by Yann Le Cun (Odile Jacob) — besides being passionate, explains in detail the math functions underlying neural networks.

**Advanced:**
- Hugging Face "Papers" page — latest scientific articles, ranked by engineer votes (helps discern importance), with comments sections.
- Stanford free online courses, such as CS224n.
- Anthropic research blog — excellent articles.

### 19.3 Apprendre par la pratique

#### Points
- In this eminently experimental domain, practice is paramount. Important to learn programming — not for the language itself (LLMs already generate working programs in any language) but to familiarize oneself with programming's mode of thought and train oneself to build algorithms. These two skills will help understand how thinking machines work. Programming isn't so difficult; many courses can help get started.
- Once bases are acquired, build projects. Follow an existing project trying to advance on your own; better yet, follow your curiosity to answer your own questions, test hypotheses, build your own tools.
- You won't need to know code on your fingertips, since an AI model can generate code for you — you only need the supervision-level understanding.
- Anecdote: the author met a doctor who built a website without any coding notions — he asked an AI to code a first version, then continued dialogue describing symptoms of the defects, correcting step-by-step to a very good result.
- AI models will help build your project, and can also be a component — you can call on Anthropic or OpenAI servers to generate text completions, read/draw images, transcribe texts.

#### Proposed projects:
- Beginner: learn to build elementary building blocks with Python.
- Beginner: follow Hugging Face "Agents Course" to familiarize with agents. Then close everything and try coding your own agent from scratch.
- Intermediate: follow the "Deep Learning Specialization" by the brilliant Andrew Ng on his platform Deeplearning.ai.
- Advanced: follow the course "Building GPT-3 from scratch" by Andrej Karpathy, or explore the GitHub repo "NanoGPT".

#### Final exhortation
- Regularly try new tools and models, keeping in mind their current capabilities are just points on an exponential growth curve; their limits are perhaps all provisional; and their competences are just a sketch of their future power.

## Conclusion (p. 169)

#### Points
- All decisions in our history — war and peace, great works and small — have been made by humans. AI's arrival marks a rupture in the fabric of history: machines come to shape the world alongside us.
- This will induce deep transformations. Some are threatening: mass unemployment, addiction to artificial paradises, exploding inequalities.
- Others are luminous: effective tutors giving every schoolchild equal chance, very high-quality healthcare, scientific progress extending healthy life, support for the elderly.
- Impossible to foresee precisely the outcome of these revolutions.
- The speed of change will give them the aspect of violent crises: work crisis, social crises, political crises.
- The coming storm may leave behind a clearer sky, but may also cast us on the reefs.
- Since it's impossible to slow down unless all world countries agree on a moratorium on AI development, we are condemned to enter the storm.
- "Alors fixons un bon cap, et tenons fermement la barre." ("So let us fix a good course, and hold firmly the helm.")
